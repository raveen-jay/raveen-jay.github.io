For the longest time, I've been fascinated with the way Donald Trump presents himself to the world. In some eyes, he's viewed as the voice of "the people", the saviour of traditional American values. However, the majority view him as a controversial and polarizing figure. One whose success is built upon his charisma and appeal to right-wing ideologies.

But how did he do it? How did he persuade the American population into supporting his distasteful beliefs? How did he develop such a strong and loyal following?

How? Through the way he spoke.

One of the many notable differences surrounding Trump's presidency is his Twitter presence. Like other prominent figures, Trump uses Twitter as a way to connect with his supporters and share his thoughts about truly anything.

Yet unlike other presidential candidates, Trump is adamant he has full control over his account. Which makes sense given his overwhelming hatred for 'Fake News' and 'the Liberal Media'. This contrasts with Obama who has a team of people running his account. Trump is also relentless in his approach to Twitter. Despite only lasting one term, Trump tweeted over double the amount Obama did, producing 16,000 tweets in four years as president.

Trump has used Twitter as a tool to broadcast his rants and attack his opponents. He uses it as a way to express his grievances with the world, like how the average person would use the platform. This definitely played a factor in Trump amassing such a devoted fanbase. Not only do they subscribe to Trump's messaging, but they also resonate with the outspoken manner in which he delivers it.

Trump and Populism

One of the many critiques of Trump's campaign has been his overt use of populism and populist language. Populism refers to a political approach that strives to appeal to "ordinary people". Populism preys on the notion that their problems are disregarded by an established elite group.

Political figures who use populism are often charismatic, and present themselves as the "voice for the people". These qualities are clearly applicable to Trump's entire campaign. Some examples of this would be his frequent use of collective pronouns: like 'our', 'we', 'us'; as well as phrases like 'the people' and 'Americans'.

The downside to using this political strategy is that is very uncompromising. It disregards the existence of differing opinions from those of "the people". It antagonizes any form of opposition. And it presents overly simplistic answers to complex problems or questions. Trump also uses populism as a way to ostracize his opponents. To draw distinctions between him and his supporters, with those who exist outside this group: Muslims, Mexicans and other minorities.

Trump's strong bond between him and his supporters, is a result of his use of populism. People find it difficult to identify and relate with someone that's different from them. So, by using the language of populism, Trump forgoes that barrier and makes his supporters feel understood and heard.

Simple, low-grade level language

The most unique part about Donald Trump's language is its lack of complexity. A study conducted across all US presidents found that Trump talks at a fourth grader's level. The lowest of the last fifteen presidents. Comparingly, Barack Obama communicates at a ninth grader's level, and George W Bush talks at a seventh grader's level.

Tools for emphasis

Another one of Donald Trumps' techniques revolves around his use of emphasis. From hyperbole to repetition, he uses all the tools available to him to draw attention to certain words.

Hyperbole means exaggeration, which Trump uses to place emphasis on particular words. Trump's language often occurs in extremes, it's either 'terrible' or 'incredible'. This extremist language heightens the exaggeration in his speech. Trump also uses intensifiers which are adverbs used to give force or emphasis. Some examples are: 'really', 'extremely' and 'enormously'. Trump will also use capitalization in his tweets to again create emphasis. It's looked at as the written equivalent to shouting, thus it's suitable for Twitter, and is another great tool to command a users attention.

However, his ultimate tool for emphasis is repetition. Trump uses repetition not only for emphasis but to also link ideas and to make a statement memorable. He also uses repetition to strengthen associations with things. For example, the repeated use of “crooked” when referring to Hillary Clinton, creates a connection between the two. Fragmented sentences and talking off-topic

The penultimate feature of Trump's language we'll be talking about, is his use of fragmented sentences. As well as his constant asides and talking off-topic. This linguistic choice doesn't feature in his tweets but appears in his speeches.

Fragmented sentences are natural in conversational dialogue, yet uncommon in formal speeches. Especially speeches that are expected to be planned, written and rehearsed. I question whether this linguistic style is intentional or just the natural, disorganized way he speaks. If you compare speeches made before he was president, with speeches he made in the past two years, it appears this is the natural way he speaks.

It's difficult to tell whether this is a deliberate choice or not. Especially difficult when his digressions are filled with absolute nonsense with no relevancy to the topic at hand. Thus, it's safe to assume that his natural tendency for disjointed sentences is amplified further for formal settings. Surprisingly, there are some persuasive elements attributed to this style of speech. Like his use of simple language, it mirrors natural human conversation, aiding in his attempts to relate with his audience. This style of speech also contrasts starkly with other presidents. Their formal dialogue is often very calculated, sophisticated and robotic for some. This deters certain people away, due to its unengaging delivery, its complexity and its stark difference from natural conversation.

Salesman-like quality

The last and most intriguing language features of Donald Trump involve his use of sales talk. Throughout his tenure as president, Trump used advertising tactics to manipulate his audience. Whether it be the slogan's he used like 'Make America Great Again', to the individual words he used.

Three of Trump's most used phrases were stolen right out of a sales and marketing textbook - “Believe me”, “you know?” and “Many people are saying”.

When Trump says "believe me", he is suggesting the idea to his audience that he knows what he's talking about, and that you should trust him. This principle known as 'justified belief' shapes the listener into believing the speaker is an expert, even if they're not. Similarly, Trump's use of "Many people are saying", implies that he's had direct conversations about the subject he's talking about. This works better than stating the claim by itself, as it portrays the claim as being more trustworthy. Lastly, Trump’s use of “you know?” helps build trust with the listener. It verbally acknowledges the listener, helps them feel a part of the conversation and educated on a subject.

I'd like to take it back to the beginning where I said - Trump is considered one of the most polarizing and controversial presidents of the century. It's hard to deny this fact, but it’s important to acknowledge the amount of intentionality and strategy behind the creation of his image and brand.

Donald Trump was aware of his audience, and targeted his language and speech to cater to said audience. Using simple language, emphasis and a salesman-like quality, to persuade the majority of the United States into voting for him. These techniques aren't only used by him, but by people who coexist across the political compass. Used as well by celebrities and popular figures who exist outside the political sphere entirely. And now that he's officially been removed from office, we need to start interrogating the words used by people like him. And weaken the power those words have over us individually, and collectively as a people.
